Research article

Remote refocus enables class-leading spatiotemporal resolution in 4D optical microscopy

Alfred Millett-Sikking1*, Nathaniel H. Thayer1, Adam Bohnert1 and Andrew G. York1

1Calico Life Sciences LLC, South San Francisco, CA 94080, USA

*Institutional email: amsikking+RR@calicolabs.com

Abstract

High resolution in space and time is often essential for studying life under a microscope. Fast 2D microscopy has been routine for decades (limited by signal levels or detector speed), but 3D is much slower, typically limited by the focusing method. Here we show that remote refocus [Botcherby 2007] removes focusing as a speed limit while adding minimal drawbacks, and we propose that remote refocus is the right technique for most high-speed 3D microscopy applications. To our knowledge, there is no commercial implementation of remote refocus, so we provide a modular high-performance design to enable others to build their own. We also present the concept, method and rules of remote refocus to help others improve or modify our design. Our design gives camera-limited speed (>4.2x108 voxels/s) and diffraction-limited resolution (<270 nm) over a user-specified volume up to 200x200x60 μm3. We demonstrate the speed and flexibility of remote refocus using a variety of live biological samples with 3D rates ranging from 2.5-50 volumes per second, 3-27x faster than standard focusing methods.

Intended audience

Microscopy users, developers, engineers and scientists interested in fast volumetric imaging at high resolution. We tried to write the article in an accessible and straighforward fashion; our aim is to lower the barrier to making a good remote refocus, and allow readers to easily replicate our design, or design and build their own with confidence.

Introduction

Remote refocus removes the primary speed limit of 3D microscopy, with minimal drawbacks

Revealing the 3D dynamics of living samples is the greatest strength of optical microscopy. To capture these dynamics, we assemble 4D spatiotemporal information from a series of 3D spatial measurements, which are in turn assembled from a series of 2D images [Fischer 2011]. 2D imaging at "video rate" or above (20-30 frames per second) has been standard in the field for decades [Inoué 1997], but adding the third dimension with standard focusing methods is comparatively slow [Lukes 2016], and still represents a challenge for those aiming to achieve the fastest volumetric frame rates at the highest resolutions [Bouchard 2015].

Light flux from the sample sets a fundamental limit on temporal resolution; even a detector with infinite speed and zero noise must wait until it detects enough photoelectrons to form a decent image [Pawley 2006]. Photoelectron-limited operation is rarely achieved due to hardware limitations, which in 2D imaging is often the camera speed (pixels per second and frames per second) and in 3D imaging is typically the axial (Z) focusing mechanism.

Many research groups have attacked this focusing speed bottleneck. Notable approaches include imaging multiple focal planes simultaneously via diffractive optics [Blanchard 2000, Prabhat 2004] or light field measurements [Levoy 2006, Broxton 2013], with recent highlights in both approaches [Abrahamsson 2013, Prevedel 2014]. Here we hope to revive a technique of similar vintage, the remote refocus (RR) [Botcherby 2007], and suggest that remote refocusing is the right way to achieve fast, high resolution volumes in most cases.

In our hands, RR eliminates the 3D bottleneck, pushing axial focusing speed beyond camera-limited speed (or photoelectron-limited speed, for dim samples). Remote refocusing's drawbacks are minimal: lower optical efficiency due to four extra lenses in the emission path, and an extended depth of field at the highest volumetric frame rates. Whilst lower efficiency is never desirable, we note that additional lenses in the emission path are tolerable and typical in many microscopy setups, such as external filter wheels, spinning disk units, etc. Elongated depth of field is either a blessing or a curse depending on the application, but only becomes relevant at the highest volumetric rates, when RR piezo settling times exceed the rolling time of our camera (see design section for details).

In contrast, multifocus [Abrahamsson 2013] and light field microscopy [Prevedel 2014] push axial focusing speed to infinity, but reduce camera-limited imaging speed. Multifocus microscopy's X, Y, and Z fields of view can't be adjusted, which wastes voxels per second unless the sample happens to perfectly fill the (fixed) imaging volume. Light field microscopy yields even fewer voxels per second than multifocus microscopy, because it measures multiple camera pixels to characterize one Nyquist-limited voxel (other drawbacks include worse resolution and mandatory post-processing). These techniques are therefore faster than RR when the bottleneck is focusing time, but slower when the bottleneck is the camera. In our experience with well-engineered fluorescence microscopes, the bottleneck is always the camera. Given the importance of fast 3D imaging, and the power and flexibility of RR, we're surprised RR isn't more popular. The history of RR shows many clever technical innovations, but few biological applications, and no commercial implementations. We speculate RR suffers from two problems: surprising pitfalls during design and construction, and a lack of advertising. We hope our design empowers builders to painlessly, confidently make their own RR, and we hope data like Figure 1 (high-resolution 3D images of C. elegans at 20 volumes per second) and Figure 2 (high-resolution 3D images of yeast flowing in a microfluidic channel at 50 volumes per second) will entice microscope users to try RR for their high-speed imaging challenges. Most of all, we hope to inspire microscope manufacturers to commercialize and disseminate remote refocus as the "gold standard" solution for fast 3D imaging problems.

The why and how of remote re-focus

The motivation for fast re-focusing to give volumetric information is clear but the advantages of ‘remote’ or indeed what this means may not be obvious. In standard microscopes focusing is achieved with the familiar z-axis motion of either the primary objective or the sample to select the image plane (Figure 3, a) ). This is conveniently simple but the relatively high inertia of these elements on a typical microscope stand presents a major drawback when agile motion is required. This can be overcome by integrating a high power z-drive actuator onto the objective whilst holding the sample stationary to avoid disturbance, but when the highest resolutions are needed an immersion medium is used which gives an unwanted mechanical coupling. This is particularly detrimental when working with sensitive samples or in a dipping regime where fast movements of the objective will often give blur or wobble artefacts (Figure 3, c) ).

The ability to re-focus remotely, without moving either the sample or the primary objective (Figure 3, b) ), can completely isolate the sample from perturbations and present unique opportunities to improve motion dynamics to give significantly faster 4D acquisitions (Figure 3, d) ). Additional benefits include the 'bolt on' nature of the module which does not interfere with the normal operation of the microscope and provides a convenient space for a fast filter wheel without the need for an additional telescope which is commonly added for this purpose.

A detailed description of RR theory is elegantly presented in the original paper [Botcherby 2007] and will not be re-visited here. We do however provide a straightforward concept, rules and simulation section in the appendix for those looking to build their own RR which highlights the potential pitfalls. We also give a concise summary of the history of the technique which may help the reader frame its use in the field of microscopy and understand how the RR version presented here fits into the story.

Results

Large diffraction limited volumes

The remote re-focus system presented in this article is designed to couple to a typical microscope stand and produce diffraction limited images using a 60x1.45 oil immersion objective (Figure 4). The z-drive included can achieve step and settle times in the 0.1-1μm range in <10 ms which is faster than the rolling time of a good sCMOS camera at full field, and so the dynamics of the system are purely photon and detector limited in this regime. A detailed description of the module can be found in the design and build sections of the appendix and so here the emphasis will be on the resulting optical performance.

The rigor of the original RR paper [Botcherby 2007], the comprehensive testing of subsequent builders [Anselmi 2011, Qi 2014] and the thorough optical design work implemented here meant that traditional and labour intensive volumetric PSF characterisation (using beads) was avoided and substituted for the very convenient Argolight SIM slide. This sample does not have subdriffactive point sources but does feature spatially stable patterns with broadband excitation/emmision bands and non bleaching linear features down to 30nm seperation. This was considered more than sufficient for evaluating the resolution and field of view of the RR and so the optical performance data is presented on this basis.

Using a standard GFP filter cube it was straighforward to image a minimally distorted, full field of view resolution target, with line pairs separated by 750nm accross the whole 200x200x60μm volume (Figure 5). Here the microscope z-drive was used to de-focus the sample and then the RR piezo was used to re-focus and take pictures and therefore scan the design volume of the instrument (see the aquisition section for details and links to the original data).

Furthermore by translating finely spaced SIM resolution lines througth the field of view we could readily resolve line features to better than 270nm accross the entire volume (Figure 6). Note that this is an upper bound on resolving power with the central volume showing <240 nm. The classic Rayleigh limit \(\frac{0.61\lambda}{NA}\) gives an expectation of ~230nm in the green and so this demonstrates excellent overall optical performance that is consistent with our expections of diffraction limited performance within the design volume (see design section for details).

To complete the testing straightforward broadband optical efficiency and stability tests were carried out giving a throughput of >73% and a mechanical drift of ~150nm/h respectively (see the design section). For a nice demonstration of the volumetric capabilty of the RR see Firgure 7 which shows a uniform 3D structure consisting of an array of rings separated by 5μm in each axis (see Full data for high resolution images).

Much faster, especially with non-rigid and delicate samples

Having established excellent optical performance, the temporal dynamics of the remote re-focus were explored using a series of standard samples: a glass slide with chrome graticule (rigid), a glass slide with agarose pad and coverslip (semi-rigid), a plastic dish with coverslip bottom (semi-rigid) and a microfluidic chip (flexible) (Figure 8). To evaluate the advantages of the RR the system was compared to a typcal microscope stand and a high peformance piezo stage insert. For each sample the microsope was used to set an initial 10 μm de-focus and then each device in turn was used to focus the sample with an approprate time interval before returning to it's initial position. The allocated time was determined in a sample independent way, either by the resulting serial communications latency for the microscope (> 100ms) or by evaluating the step and settle time of the piezo devices (15-50ms) using internal sensors (see the aquisition section in the appendix for details).

For the graticule and agarose slides all three z-drives were able to reach focus in the allocated times, with the microscope showing significant xy motion but the piezo stage insert and RR options both focusing as expected. Because these samples are stiff and well clamped the main benefit of the RR in this regime is the physical decoupling of the fast z-drive from the microscope which allows a wide range of actuators to be considered. In this design the <15 ms step and settle of the RR (10μm jump) is achieved by using a bulky piezo unit that does not fit on every microscope platform and occupies a large space that interferes with the nosepiece automation and neighbouring objectives. Because the RR piezo unit is large, powerful and specifically optimised for the RR objective it offers an immediate 3x gain in speed compared to the other methods we tested, regardless of the sample. It should also be noted that another major practical benefit of this architecture is that the RR piezo, camera and light source can all be triggered and controlled by high speed analogue signals which aviods the communication latency that often cripples good temporal performance in focusing.

For the dish and microfluidic chip samples the advantages of the RR are substantial. The microscope is able to reach focus on the dish by virture of being ~7x slower, which gives the oil and sample just enough time to settle (although the xy motion is still noticable). The stage insert however is only ~3x slower and so extra settle time must be added to the acquisition to compensate for the motion coupled to the sample and reach a sharp focus. With the flexible microfluidic chip the coupling of the oil is more detrimental and incapacitates both the microscope and stage inserts ability to focus on the normal time scales (50-110ms). In a separate test the settle time was found to be ~400ms which is ~27x slower than the RR. The RR naturally performs as well with the dish and microfluidc chip as with any other sample giving a speed advantage in the 3-27x range for the samples demonstrated here and larger benefits expected in a water dipping regime where this technique could revolutionise z stack acquisition.

Dynamic 4D biology

Having established both excellent optomechanical performance of the remote re-focus and clear speed advantages of using it with a range of standard samples some readily available living organisms were identified and imaged. Here we show the RR in action with waterborne Protozoa freely moving in a 3D volume at up to 14 volumes/s (Figure 9), C. elegans freely roaming around on an agarose pad at up to 20 volumes/s (Figure 1) and yeast cells flowing in microfluidic channels at up to 50 volumes/s (Figures 2 and 9).

Discussion

Remote re-focus gives the highest spatiotemporal resolution in many applications

Many applications of high resolution optical microscopy of live organisms could benefit from a decoupling of the focusing mechanism from the sample space by using the remote re-focus method, which is particularly relevant when sensitive or flexible mounting of the sample is inherent and fast 4D acquisitions are required. Here we clearly outline the concept, method and rules for those wishing to design and build their own RR system and provide a brief overview of the history of the technique.

A high performance RR arrangement is presented that may be easily replicated with a detailed discussion of the associated design choices and alignment procedure. The system is shown to have excellent optical properties over a huge 200x200x60μm volume with good corresponding mechanical stability and fast motion dynamics that result in a maximally performant photon limited system when using a modern sCMOS camera at full field. The benefits of RR are demonstrated using typical samples where it clearly outperforms standard focusing methods with speed gains in the 3-27x range. The imaging capabilities of the system are then shown through a series of live biological samples with speeds ranging from 2.5-50 volumes/s.
